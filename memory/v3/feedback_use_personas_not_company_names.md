When framing decisions, design discussions, or panel debates, **don't lead with company names** like Sodexo, G4S, ISS, Compass, Stripe, Linear, Razorpay, Phonepe, etc. Lead with concrete scenarios using the persona names from master plan §O: Mr. Reddy (owner), Mrs. Kavitha (HR), Suresh / Vinod (supervisors), Mukesh / Ravi / Lakshmi / Ramesh / Priya (workers).

**Why:** Founder explicitly called this out 2026-05-09: "why are you always saying sudeox this and that why not think from scenarios whatever we need." Name-dropping is MBA-deck framing — it sounds authoritative but it's actually substituting brand recognition for real-world reasoning. The founder's master plan has been built ground-up with persona scenarios. Reverting to enterprise-name framing undoes that discipline.

Persona-framed reasoning has two advantages:
1. **Forces specificity** — "Mr. Reddy with 80 workers and 18 sites" vs "tier-2 SMB segment" is the difference between a real scenario and a vague claim.
2. **Reduces cognitive load** — the founder built the personas; he remembers them. Sodexo's internal practices are abstract claims he can't verify or use.

**How to apply:**
- Open every panel debate with a concrete scenario in Mr. Reddy's company (or another persona's). Example: "Mr. Reddy's HR signs up a new client at 6pm Friday. They need cleaning Monday."
- If a panel member's bio mentions Sodexo / G4S / Stripe etc, you can cite that bio (it's part of why that voice is on the panel) but the SCENARIO must be the persona's, not the panel member's previous employer.
- When justifying a design choice, show what happens in the scenario — not what Sodexo does.
- If you genuinely need an enterprise-scale point (e.g., scale concerns), frame it as "at 100 customers, 3 of them will look like X-scenario" — not "Sodexo does X."
- The only acceptable mention of an enterprise name is when explicitly answering a founder question about what a specific company does. Even then, lead with the scenario, mention the company once, return to the scenario.

This is a discipline lock, not a one-time correction.
